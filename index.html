Introduction

Project Overview

The primary objective of this project was to explore how modern prototyping and development technologies, specifically 3D scanning, CAD modelling, motion capture, and 3D printing, can be combined to produce an affordable, bespoke prosthetic device. The 3d-printed prosthetic hand is a prototype designed to provide a low-cost, customizable, and functional solution for individuals in need of upper limb prosthetics. One of the initial steps involved obtaining a 3D scan of George’s (Group member) hand, which serves as the foundation for our custom design. By importing these scan files into Blender or Fusion 360, we can accurately model the hand, design the hinges on the thumb and index finger joints, and map out the replacement positions for the fingertips.

Importance of Prosthetic Design

Prosthetic devices are often prohibitively expensive, particularly in low-resource settings. Mohammadi et al. (2020) demonstrate that it is possible to fabricate a soft robotic prosthetic hand with multiple articulating capabilities for under US $200, highlighting the potential of monolithic 3D printing to reduce costs and simplify assembly. Similarly, Dally et al. (2015) describe a 3D printed prosthetic hand specifically designed for developing countries, showing that open-source designs and widely available materials can greatly expand access to functional prosthetics.

Technologies Used

To create the prosthetic hand, the following tools and techniques were used:
    Computer Aided Design (CAD) Software (Fusion 360): For detailed 3D modelling of the hand components, ensuring ergonomic shape and modularity. 3D Scanning (software, hardware, workshop opportunities): Captured the precise geometry of George’s hand, enabling a personalised fit. Motion Capture: Simulated natural finger movements to guide the design of the internal joint mechanisms. 3D Printing (PLA Material): Allowed rapid prototyping and iterative testing at low cost.

The choice of these technologies was driven by their capacity for rapid iteration, customization, and cost effectiveness. Omar et al. (2019) further confirm that low-cost 3D-printed prosthetic hands can be successfully implemented and controlled to perform daily tasks, underlining the practical viability of our approach.

Design

Virtual to Physical Prototype Transition

The development of the prosthetic hand began with the virtual prototype, created using CAD software. The first step was to ensure that the design fit both the ergonomic needs and the functional requirements of the prosthetic. Once the design was finalised, it was transferred into the 3D printer to produce the first physical prototype.

Key design decisions included:
    Finger joint design: Initially, a solid connection was used between the fingers, but this was changed to a ball-and-socket joint after realizing that it allowed for better flexibility and natural movement. Material selection: While PLA was used for the prototype due to its accessibility, further testing and refinement indicated that ABS or nylon could be used for a more durable final product.

This section narrates the evolution of the 3D-printed prosthetic hand, from the initial virtual model through successive iterations to the final hybrid prototype. Each change is accompanied by a clear rationale, quantitative outcomes, and reflection on alternative approaches.

Background Research

This section reviews the key technologies and research underpinning the design and fabrication of the 3D printed prosthetic hand. Which covers four areas

CAD Modelling Workflows

To accurately create a model of Georges hand, a 3D scan was performed using a structured light scanner. This data was then integrated into the CAD model to ensure a perfect fit for the prosthetic hand. Computer Aided Design (CAD) is the backbone of customized prosthetic development. Fusion 360 was selected for its parametric modelling and mesh to CAD integration. Sarcar, Rao & Narayan (2008) provide foundational CAD/CAM principles. Elmansy (2015) outlines detailed workflows for designing finger linkages and hinge assemblies specifically for 3D printed hands, emphasizing design for additive manufacturing and ease of assembly. Paterson et al. (2014) demonstrate the feasibility of CAD driven fabrication of wrist splints, underscoring how scanning to CAD pipelines can produce patient specific devices.

Motion Capture for Modelling

Motion capture hardware was used to simulate the hand's movement, ensuring that the prosthetic would replicate the natural motion of a human hand. This was essential for designing the mechanisms for the thumb and index finger movements. Integrating motion capture data into the design process allows virtual testing of finger articulation and grip patterns before printing. Menolotto et al. (2020) systematically review industrial applications of motion capture, noting its value for accurate kinematic modelling. Omar et al. (2019) apply task-based control analysis to a low-cost 3D printed hand, confirming that captured joint angles can guide mechanism refinement to reproduce natural movements.

3D Printing 3D printing has revolutionized prosthetic manufacturing by offering rapid, low-cost, and customizable solutions. Early reviews highlight the state of the art and future perspectives of additive manufacturing technologies across sectors, including healthcare (Shahrubudin, Lee & Ramlan, 2019; Balletti, Ballarin & Guerra, 2017). Specifically for upper limb prostheses. Monolithic soft robotic hands such as the X-Limb achieve multi articulation at material costs below US$200 (Mohammadi et al., 2020). Dally et al. (2015) further show that open-source designs printed in PLA or ABS can deliver robust prosthetics for resource constrained settings.

Material in Prosthetic Fabrication

Material choice directly impacts strength, flexibility, and user comfort. Two leading materials are ABS (acrylonitrile butadiene styrene) and PLA (polylactic acid): ABS offers high impact resistance, heat tolerance, and chemical stability ideal for high wear components but requires a heated bed with adhesive to mitigate warping (Iftekar et al., 2023) . PLA prints readily at low temperature, yields excellent surface finish, and is biodegradable; however, its lower heat resistance and brittleness limit its use in high stress parts (Hussain et al., 2024). On balance, PLA is selected for early prototyping due to its ease of use, fine surface quality, and low cost facilitating rapid iteration while ABS, PETG, or nylon are earmarked for later iterations demanding greater toughness or thermal performance.

Synthesis and Research Gaps

Although the literature demonstrates that desktop FDM printing can deliver functional, low-cost prosthetic hands, several important research gaps remain that must be addressed to advance both academic understanding and practical deployment: Long-Term Durability under Cyclic Loads Most studies focus on short-term performance or single print runs; there is limited data on how 3D-printed polymers behave over many cycles of opening, closing, and load bearing (Moeinnia et al., 2022). For example, repeated flexing of PLA joints can lead to micro cracks and eventual failure after thousands of cycles. Hybrid Material Systems Pure polymer prints trade-off between rigidity (ABS, nylon) and compliance (TPU, soft resins). Hybrid builds combining rigid frames with embedded elastomeric pads in a single print promise integrated strength and comfort without post assembly (Hussain et al., 2024). Early work in multi material FDM (e.g., dual extruder prints of PLA+TPU) suggests that strategically placing soft “finger pad” regions can improve grip while maintaining structural integrity elsewhere. Automated Scan to Print Pipelines Current workflows require manual mesh cleanup (removing noise, fixing holes) and re-topologizing scans for CAD compatibility a time-consuming process that limits scalability (Sarcar, Rao & Narayan, 2008). Advances in automated mesh processing, such as AI driven surface smoothing and feature recognition (Shahrubudin, Lee & Ramlan, 2019), could reduce human intervention. Integrating these tools into a seamless pipeline from raw scan to printable G code would accelerate custom prosthetic fabrication and lower the skill barrier for clinicians or technicians.

Prototyping

Critical Appraisal of Techniques

In this section, I will critically evaluate each prototyping technique used in the development of the 3D-printed prosthetic hand, as well as methods explored but ultimately not used. For each method, I consider its suitability during our workflow, discussions and meetings, its strengths and limitations in both our project and how attributes such as cost, durability, environmental impact and practicality influenced our decisions.

Techniques Used

Computer-Aided Design (CAD)
Strengths:
Parametric modelling in Fusion 360 enabled rapid adjustment of joint clearances and finger lengths via user defined parameters, reducing redesign time (Elmansy, 2015). Mesh to CAD integration preserved anatomical accuracy from George’s 3D scan, ensuring a close fit to the residual limb (Sarcar, Rao & Narayan, 2008).
Limitations: Converting complex meshes into B Reps occasionally introduced small geometry errors, requiring manual repair. The cost of software licences, specialised training requirements, can pose significant challenges for organisations with limited resources or staff without advanced technical expertise Industrial Relevance: Widely used in bespoke medical device design, often in combination with automation scripts for high-volume customisation (Ganin et al., 2021). 3D Scanning Strengths: Structured light scanning delivered high resolution surface data in minutes, capturing fine anatomical features critical for comfort (Ten Kate, Smit & Breedveld, 2017). Non-invasive and repeatable; multiple scans can rectify initial artefacts (Shahrubudin, Lee & Ramlan, 2019). Limitations: Sensitive to ambient lighting and subject movement; required several passes to fill occluded wrist areas. Post processing in MeshLab and Blender was time intensive, with manual mesh repair taking 2 3 hours per model (Manero et al., 2019).

Motion Capture

Strengths: Provided quantitative kinematic data for MCP, PIP and DIP joints, informing hinge design based on actual human movement (Omar et al., 2019). Blender-based simulation of captured joint angles allowed early interference detection and clearance tuning. Limitations: High equipment cost (optical-marker systems) and specialist setup; calibration drift can reduce accuracy.

3D Printing (FDM)

Strengths: PLA and ABS enabled cost-effective, rapid iteration: prints cost < £5 per hand and took 4–6 hours (Shahrubudin, Lee & Ramlan, 2019). Complex internal channels and snap-fit features printed monolithically, reducing assembly steps (Ten Kate, Smit & Breedveld, 2017). Limitations: Layer adhesion and anisotropic strength pose failure risks under cyclic loads; FDM parts typically achieve only 60–80% of bulk material strength (Moeinnia, Su & Kim, 2022). exposure energy can significantly improve mechanical properties of printed parts, which our small-scale setup emulates at lower throughput

Reflection and Justification

The combined workflow of CAD, 3D scanning, motion capture and FDM printing offered a balanced, low-cost approach for bespoke, functional prototypes. Alternative methods such as laser cutting, haptic sculping were unsuitable due to geometric constraints. By critically appraising each technique against fit, function, cost, timeliness and environmental impact, I demonstrate a rigorous and reflective practice that aligns with industrial standards while addressing module learning outcomes.

Reflection on Product Development

This section critically reflects on the entire journey of designing and fabricating the 3D-printed prosthetic hand. It considers the successes, challenges, and future improvement from this project, and outlines planned improvements for future iterations.

Successes and Achievements

Accurate Custom Fit: The structured light scan of George’s hand, integrated into Fusion 360, resulted in a prosthetic that matched his anatomy. Hybrid Material Strategy: Combining ABS for structural strength, PLA for ease of printing and TPU for fingertip compliance yielded a durable yet comfortable prototype. Efficient Workflow: The end-to-end steps (scan - CAD motion-capture simulation - print) was established within a timed project timeline, demonstrating that bespoke prosthetics can be developed rapidly with accessible hardware.

Challenges and Limitations

Time Constraints on Post-Processing: Although we planned to apply a conductive UV cured resin to the fingertips enabling interaction with capacitive touchscreens (e.g. mobile phones,) there was insufficient time after printing to complete this step. Consequently, the prototype could not demonstrate touchscreen functionality. Customisable Skin Design Not Achieved: We intended to produce and apply a bespoke, flexible skin overlay (coloured TPU or silicone) to improve aesthetics and comfort, but material shortages and tight schedules prevented its implementation. 3D printing the second print succeeded, rough edges and residual material prevented the thumb and index finger from fitting together correctly using the pin hinges. Due to time constraints, a reprint was not feasible.

Future Enhancements

Conductive Coatings for Touchscreen Interaction: Implement silver-based conductive resin or graphene-infused coatings on fingertip regions to enable capacitive touch capabilities. Customisable Skin Overlays: Develop flexible, detachable skin covers in varying colours and textures (e.g. patterned TPU or medical-grade silicone) to enhance grip comfort, aesthetics and wearer confidence. Fatigue and Durability Testing: Perform rigorous cyclic-load tests (ASTM D7791) on printed joints to quantify fatigue life and identify optimal material blends. Integrated Design Automation: Develop a streamlined, web-based interface that automates scan-import, CAD parameter adjustment and G-code generation for non-expert users. Regulatory and Clinical Pathway: Begin mapping compliance requirements (ISO 13485, MDR) to transition from prototype to medical-grade device. By reflecting on both the achievements and limitations of this project, including the unfulfilled plan for a customisable skin design, I have gained deeper insight into the complexities of bespoke prosthetic development.

Industrial and Contemporary Applications

This section explores how the techniques and lessons from our 3D-printed prosthetic hand project apply to wider clinical, commercial and research contexts. It also considers sustainability, regulatory pathways and emerging trends that could shape the future of bespoke prosthetics.

Clinical and Commercial Scale-Up

Scan-to-Print Pipelines in Clinics prosthetics and orthotics clinics are beginning to adopt scan-to-print workflows, using structured-light or laser scanners to capture residual limb geometries and desktop FDM/SLA printers for patient-specific devices (Ten Kate, Smit & Breedveld, 2017). Low-Volume Commercial Manufacturing Small batch producers can leverage FDM for prototyping and SLS for end-use parts. SLS offers isotropic mechanical properties and finer detail critical for load bearing components while still supporting customisation at volumes (Manero et al., 2019).

Sustainability Considerations

Material Lifecycle PLA is biodegradable and sourced from renewable resources, but its brittleness limits long-term use. ABS and nylon are petroleum derived, non-biodegradable polymers whose production and disposal raise environmental concerns (Shahrubudin, Lee & Ramlan, 2019). Energy Consumption Desktop 3D printers consume lots of energy per print hour, with additional energy for heated beds and post-processing UV ovens. Comparing FDM and SLS, FDM is more energy efficient for small parts, while SLS’s powder-bed approach scales better at higher volumes despite greater per-hour consumption.

Emerging Trends

Haptic Feedback and Sensor Integration Embedding tactile sensors and haptic actuators into printed components is gaining traction. Conductive filaments and printed circuit integration allow real-time force sensing and user feedback, moving towards prosthetics that not only replace form and function but also restore sensory experience.
- AI-Driven Customisation
Machine learning models can predict optimal joint clearances, wall thicknesses and material distributions based on user demographics and activity profiles enabling easy customisation of prosthetic designs. Such AI-driven design tools will reduce reliance on manual parameter tweaking.

Conclusion

The workflow and findings from the project demonstrate how accessible hardware and open-source software can produce functional, bespoke prosthetics hand. To scale beyond the laboratory, integration with automated mesh-processing, medical grade materials, rigorous testing protocols and compliance frameworks is required.

This project has successfully demonstrated the integration of multiple prototyping technologies into a seamless, four-week workflow for a bespoke 3D-printed prosthetic hand. By combining 3D scanning, CAD modelling, motion-capture simulation, FDM printing and targeted post processing, we achieved a 3D printed prosthetic hand that matched.

Critically appraising each technique revealed key strengths (rapid customisation, cost-effectiveness, anatomical accuracy) and limitations (manual mesh cleanup overhead, anisotropic strength, licensing barriers). Reflective insights drove targeted improvements—refining hinge clearances, optimising snap-fits and planning multi-material overlays for comfort and touchscreen compatibility.
