Introduction

Project Overview

The primary objective of this project was to explore how modern prototyping and development technologies, specifically 3D scanning, CAD modelling, motion capture, and 3D printing, can be combined to produce an affordable, bespoke prosthetic device. The 3d-printed prosthetic hand is a prototype designed to provide a low-cost, customizable, and functional solution for individuals in need of upper limb prosthetics. One of the initial steps involved obtaining a 3D scan of George’s (Group member) hand, which serves as the foundation for our custom design. By importing these scan files into Blender or Fusion 360, we can accurately model the hand, design the hinges on the thumb and index finger joints, and map out the replacement positions for the fingertips.

Importance of Prosthetic Design

Prosthetic devices are often prohibitively expensive, particularly in low-resource settings. Mohammadi et al. (2020) demonstrate that it is possible to fabricate a soft robotic prosthetic hand with multiple articulating capabilities for under US $200, highlighting the potential of monolithic 3D printing to reduce costs and simplify assembly. Similarly, Dally et al. (2015) describe a 3D printed prosthetic hand specifically designed for developing countries, showing that open-source designs and widely available materials can greatly expand access to functional prosthetics.

Technologies Used

To create the prosthetic hand, the following tools and techniques were used:
    Computer Aided Design (CAD) Software (Fusion 360): For detailed 3D modelling of the hand components, ensuring ergonomic shape and modularity. 3D Scanning (software, hardware, workshop opportunities): Captured the precise geometry of George’s hand, enabling a personalised fit. Motion Capture: Simulated natural finger movements to guide the design of the internal joint mechanisms. 3D Printing (PLA Material): Allowed rapid prototyping and iterative testing at low cost.

The choice of these technologies was driven by their capacity for rapid iteration, customization, and cost effectiveness. Omar et al. (2019) further confirm that low-cost 3D-printed prosthetic hands can be successfully implemented and controlled to perform daily tasks, underlining the practical viability of our approach.

Design

Virtual to Physical Prototype Transition

The development of the prosthetic hand began with the virtual prototype, created using CAD software. The first step was to ensure that the design fit both the ergonomic needs and the functional requirements of the prosthetic. Once the design was finalised, it was transferred into the 3D printer to produce the first physical prototype. Key design decisions included:

    Finger joint design: Initially, a solid connection was used between the fingers, but this was changed to a ball-and-socket joint after realizing that it allowed for better flexibility and natural movement. Material selection: While PLA was used for the prototype due to its accessibility, further testing and refinement indicated that ABS or nylon could be used for a more durable final product.
This section narrates the evolution of the 3D-printed prosthetic hand, from the initial virtual model through successive iterations to the final hybrid prototype. Each change is accompanied by a clear rationale, quantitative outcomes, and reflection on alternative approaches.

CAD Modelling Workflows

To accurately create a model of Georges hand, a 3D scan was performed using a structured light scanner. This data was then integrated into the CAD model to ensure a perfect fit for the prosthetic hand. Computer Aided Design (CAD) is the backbone of customized prosthetic development. Fusion 360 was selected for its parametric modelling and mesh to CAD integration. Sarcar, Rao & Narayan (2008) provide foundational CAD/CAM principles. Elmansy (2015) outlines detailed workflows for designing finger linkages and hinge assemblies specifically for 3D printed hands, emphasizing design for additive manufacturing and ease of assembly. Paterson et al. (2014) demonstrate the feasibility of CAD driven fabrication of wrist splints, underscoring how scanning to CAD pipelines can produce patient specific devices.

Motion Capture for Modelling

Motion capture hardware was used to simulate the hand's movement, ensuring that the prosthetic would replicate the natural motion of a human hand. This was essential for designing the mechanisms for the thumb and index finger movements. Integrating motion capture data into the design process allows virtual testing of finger articulation and grip patterns before printing. Menolotto et al. (2020) systematically review industrial applications of motion capture, noting its value for accurate kinematic modelling. Omar et al. (2019) apply task-based control analysis to a low-cost 3D printed hand, confirming that captured joint angles can guide mechanism refinement to reproduce natural movements.

3D Printing 3D printing has revolutionized prosthetic manufacturing by offering rapid, low-cost, and customizable solutions. Early reviews highlight the state of the art and future perspectives of additive manufacturing technologies across sectors, including healthcare (Shahrubudin, Lee & Ramlan, 2019; Balletti, Ballarin & Guerra, 2017). Specifically for upper limb prostheses. Monolithic soft robotic hands such as the X-Limb achieve multi articulation at material costs below US$200 (Mohammadi et al., 2020). Dally et al. (2015) further show that open-source designs printed in PLA or ABS can deliver robust prosthetics for resource constrained settings.
